q1 = read.csv("student-mat[1].csv", sep=";", header=TRUE)
q2 = read.csv("student-por[1].csv", sep=";", header=TRUE)
q3 = inner_join(q1,q2,by=c("school","sex","age","address","famsize","Pstatus","Medu","Fedu","Mjob","Fjob","reason","nursery","internet"))
#reads in the csv's and merges them

num.cols <- c("Medu", "Fedu", "famrel.x", "G3.x", "G3.y")
q3[num.cols] <- sapply(q3[num.cols], as.numeric)
#makes numeric
q3$G3.avg <- (q3$G3.x + q3$G3.y / 2)
#takes averages
summary2 <- function(col3) {
#function takes in a column name
plot2 <- ggplot(q3, mapping = aes(x=q3[[col3]], y=G3.avg, group=q3[[col3]])) +
  geom_boxplot() +
  labs(x=col3, y="Average Grade", title="Average Grades by Variable")
#making a boxplot for the column

df2<-q3 %>%
  group_by(q3[[col3]]) %>%
  summarize(Mean = mean(G3.avg), Number = n(), Median = median(G3.avg), Standard_Deviation = sd(G3.avg))
#storing a dataframe for the column with summary stats for it
colnames(df2)[1] <- col3
#renaming


if(typeof(q3[[col3]]) == "double") {
  #make a scatterplot for numeric columns
plot3 <- ggplot(df2, aes(x=df2[[col3]], y=Mean)) +
  geom_line() +
  geom_point() +
  labs(x=col3, y="Average Grade", title="Grade means of each group")


return(list(plot2, knitr::kable(df2), plot3))
#numeric columns return the boxplot, the dataframe, and the scatterplot
}

else {
  return(list(plot2, knitr::kable(df2)))
  #non-numeric columns return the boxplot and the dataframe
}

}

REPORT:

I analyzed a data set on secondary education schools in Portugal. Every row is an individual student, ages ranging from 15-22, filled with lots of information about them, collected by using schools reports and questionnaires. There was no missing data (!). In particular, their grades out of 30 in each of Portuguese and Mathematics are shown. For my analysis, I took the average of these two columns as my dependent variable (G3.avg). The data sets are separate for Portuguese and Mathematics, but in the documentation defines 382 students that occur in both data sets, and a method to merge them on several specified columns, which I replicated here. The variables that were used as keys for the merge are: (school,sex,age,address,famsize,Pstatus,Medu,Fedu,Mjob,Fjob,reason,nursery,internet). This would create indentical duplicates, ending in a .x and a .y, but interestingly each had a few FALSEs when I set say famrel.x == famrel.y, so I note this as an error in my research. There were many variables, but in particular I wanted to see how the family life variables affected the dependent variable of the Portuguese-Math grade average. Thus my research question was what aspects of family life affect student performance, and how?

I cleaned the data a little bit, as some obviously numeric columns were not actually coded as numeric after the merge, so I set them as such. I then wrote a very long function to provide insight into my variables of interest.

Several variables were of interest for this. Mothers Educational Level coded as Medu was on a 0-4 numerical scale: (0 - none, 1 - primary education (4th grade), 2 5th to 9th grade, 3 secondary education or 4 higher education). Fathers Educational Level Fedu was the same. I took the sum of these to create Pedu (P for parents). The importance of the mothers educational level on grades increased steadily from 1-4, whereas the effect of the Fathers educational level would sort of level out after the 5th-9th grade category. Pedu would likewise level out after the sum of these hit six, after increasing about 1-1.5 grade points per educational level to that point. famrel.x measured the quality of family relationships (numeric: from 1 - very bad to 5 - excellent). This had a very small, but noticeably linear, effect on grades. Mjob and Fjob grouped the professions of the students mother and father, respectively, into: (teacher, health care related, civil services (e.g.administrative or police), at_home or other). It appears that teacher and health jobs correlated with the highest scores for their children, while at-home and other were on the lower ends.

Interestingly, whether or not a student received family support had no bearing on the students grades, and students with aprents living apart actually had slightly higher grades than their peers with parents living together. However, when we break this up by sex, we observe an interesting phenomenon. Female students with parents living apart score just under, a hair under the overall average, whereas male students with parents living apart score over 20 on average! Overall, however, the difference in grades between males and females is indescernible. Especially as this is disparate impact, perhaps it should be a public policy goal to ensure that female students with separated parents are still receiving the necessary support to succeed. In general, however, it is clear that the biggest causes of grade increases that I have identified include the level of educational attainment of ones parents (although particularly for fathers, the importance of this can level off) as well as the type of job your parents have. These two variables are likely correlated, in fact. It is surprising that the level of support and the level of relationship with ones family were not more important in predicting grades. Perhaps this points to limitations in my data. This is only two public schools in Portugal, so it is difficult to say how generalizable it is to everyone. Furthermore, perhaps the sample size was an issue, as you can note in the dataframes that the standard deviations are too large to claim statistical significance in almost all cases. As well, maybe the original data collection methods itself were not perfect.
